Case Study · 2023

Robots in
Public Space

Interactive Research Website — translating complex speculative robotics research into an immersive, visually compelling web experience for a Good Design Award submission.

Web Design Information Architecture Interaction Design No-Code Development

Role

Sole Web Designer

Team

Researchers, 3D Modellers, Designers

Context

Wonderlab — Industry Placement

Output

Published Website

Background

How can we design robots to enhance public spaces?

This question drove the Robots in Public Space project at Wonderlab, where researchers explored how robots could integrate into everyday environments. As the project grew, it required a broader reach — and a submission for the Australian Good Design Award. That meant creating a website capable of translating complex research into an interactive experience that would engage the general public, impress academics, and stand out to award judges.

As sole web designer, I owned every part of this: information architecture, visual and interaction design, cross-functional communication with researchers and 3D modellers, no-code development, and project timeline coordination.

Understanding the Project

Before designing, I had to understand the research from the inside.

Rather than jumping straight into design, I participated in a speculative robotics workshop — a day of digital activities and hands-on robot design using everyday materials and AR-based interaction. This gave me firsthand exposure to the research world I'd be communicating, and helped me ask better questions when working with the team.

Challenges

Real constraints that shaped every design decision.

Limited Stakeholder Availability

Researchers were deep in their own work. Design decisions had to be made independently, with async feedback loops that required clear communication and well-presented options.

Evolving Research Content

The research content continued to evolve as the website was being built. The structure needed to be flexible enough to accommodate updates without breaking the design.

No Developer, No Code Budget

The entire site had to be built without a developer and without a coding budget — meaning a no-code tool was required that could still deliver design freedom and interaction capabilities.

Clarity Meets Engagement

The audience spanned general public, academics, and award judges. The design had to make complex speculative research feel accessible, compelling, and visually impressive — all at once.

Process & Collaboration

Flexible, outcome-focused — blending research, design, and feedback in a continuous loop.

Rather than following a traditional Double Diamond framework, I applied a user-centred mindset asking: "What would help the audience understand and enjoy this speculative research?" This kept the work grounded without rigidity.

I created an initial presentation summarising project understanding, design possibilities, and key goals — giving the team a clear picture before a single frame was designed. From there, I maintained momentum through weekly visual progress updates, multiple design variations for faster decision-making, and well-documented review sessions.

For content structure, I suggested a shared Google Docs template for the team to shape and refine their research submissions — recognising that effective collaboration sometimes means adapting to tools others are more familiar with.

Visual Direction

Urban complexity meets speculative clarity.

I developed a mind map exploring visual ideas, then created a mood board combining "crowded public spaces" imagery with "clean, bold typography" — capturing both the complexity of urban life and the speculative nature of robots integrating within it.

Moodboard — blending urban density with bold, structured type.

Design Iteration

Three rounds of refinement before we got it right.

1st Iteration

Monotone colour palette. The team felt it was "too flat and didn't reflect the speculative nature of the research." Visually safe — but the content needed more energy.

2nd Iteration

Modular layout with embedded 3D robot models. Feedback: "navigation was unclear" and "structure felt confusing to users." The 3D elements added richness but the information hierarchy needed work.

3rd Iteration — The final direction.

Introduced a cityscape landing visual with scroll-triggered animation — a fading Melbourne view revealing the core research statement — for a more immersive, cinematic opening. This resolved the navigation confusion by leading users naturally through the content.

Final iteration — scroll-triggered cityscape revealing the research statement.

Development

Building with Framer — the right balance of freedom and feasibility.

After comparing no-code tools, I selected Framer as the most cost-effective and flexible solution — offering the right balance between design freedom, interaction capabilities, and ease of maintenance. This meant the research team could update content without needing a designer every time.

Scroll-triggered interactions and embedded 3D models in the final website.

Impact

Successfully launched and submitted for the Australian Good Design Award.

🏆 Website successfully launched and submitted for the Australian Good Design Award.
✓ Stakeholders satisfied with how clearly and visually the research was communicated to a broad audience.

Reflections

Leading with initiative under real-world constraints.

This project taught me how to lead with initiative under real-world constraints — and reinforced my belief that good design goes beyond visuals. It's about making complex information approachable and engaging, for audiences who may never have encountered speculative robotics research before.

Owning the Full Process

Taking responsibility for every part — from architecture to development — forced me to make decisions with confidence and see the work through from concept to live site.

Communicating Design to Non-Designers

Working with researchers meant translating design decisions into terms that connected to their goals, not my process. Weekly visual updates helped build trust without requiring constant meetings.

Iteration as a Tool, Not a Sign of Failure

Three full design iterations felt daunting at the time, but each round refined the experience in ways that a single draft never could have. Feedback is part of the craft.

Adapting to Team Constraints

Effective collaboration sometimes means meeting people where they are — suggesting Google Docs over Figma for content structure because it was the tool the researchers already knew.
