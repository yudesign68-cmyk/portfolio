Case Study · 2024

Mediphin

Portable Health Assistant for International Students — designing a smart device and kiosk system that helps international students navigate Melbourne's healthcare system with confidence.

Healthcare Mobile Design User Research Service Design

Role

UX & UI Designer

Team

Group research, solo design

Context

Academic Project

Output

Design Proposal + Prototype

Background

Even buying medicine feels like navigating a maze.

This project aimed to enhance the shopping experience for international students in Melbourne — a rapidly growing community facing unique challenges. Pharmacy shopping, in particular, emerged as a key pain point due to language barriers and unfamiliar healthcare systems. We focused on Chemist Warehouse, the most widely accessible pharmacy chain for students in Melbourne.

Imagine being in a new country, where large pharmacies are overwhelming and confusing. The lack of accessible information in native languages leaves students anxious and unsupported. Alone in an unfamiliar environment, they struggle to make confident healthcare decisions — risking disruption to their studies and well-being.

User Research

To design for real needs, we had to start with real stories.

We set out to understand the full experience of international students at Chemist Warehouse — from what drives their health decisions to the emotional challenges they face during their shopping journey.

Research Objectives

01

Identify the needs, motivations, and expectations of international students when shopping for health-related products.

02

Map the shopping journey and touchpoints at Chemist Warehouse, from information search to purchase.

03

Explore students' experiences and emotions while shopping for health-related products.

04

Pinpoint pain points and challenges in the shopping journey.

05

Uncover future expectations for an improved shopping experience.

Target User

International students aged 18–35, from various nationalities, living in Australia for less than 3 years. Health-conscious and eager to adapt — regularly visiting Chemist Warehouse as part of their wellness routine, using smartphones as their primary tool for communication and research.

Methodology

To hear their stories, we went straight to the source.

In-Depth Interviews

5 interviews with international students who had recently purchased health-related products at Chemist Warehouse. A structured interview guide was prepared, with audio recordings and photographs taken for data collection.

Store Observations

To validate the interviews, we observed real shopping experiences through two rounds. First, a general store observation at Chemist Warehouse Melbourne CBD — noting layout, product shelving, customer navigation, and staff engagement. Second, we shadowed one interviewee recreating their recent shopping experience, video-recording their journey to analyse real-time interactions.

Data Analysis

We transformed raw conversations into clear patterns using affinity mapping — grouping observations into key themes. This process uncovered recurring struggles and unmet needs, which became the foundation for our user personas and empathy maps.

Affinity mapping process — grouping qualitative findings into key themes.

Key Insights

Three core pain points emerged, shaping how we approached the design.

Pain Point 01

Language Barriers Complicate Shopping

Many international students struggle to understand product labels and descriptions, leading to longer shopping times and uncertainty about what they're purchasing.

Pain Point 02

Limited Access to Healthcare Advice

Students need quick and clear medical guidance when purchasing health-related products. A lack of pharmacist availability and confusing product information makes decision-making difficult.

Pain Point 03

Difficulty Finding Reliable Information

Health is a top priority — students actively seek trustworthy sources but struggle to find information that is accurate, accessible, and in their native language.

The Solution

From arrival to daily life, Mediphin supports students with confidence and care.

Mediphin is a smart, portable device that empowers international students in Melbourne by delivering essential health support. It tracks daily activity and well-being — sleep, movement, stress levels — and translates this data into personalised health insights. By connecting with pharmacy kiosks, it enables students to receive real-time, contextual medical guidance.

Real-time Monitoring

The portable device continuously analyses health data and alerts users to potential risks. Provides directions to the pharmacy and assists in navigating items within the store.

Seamless Interaction

The kiosk and portable device automatically exchange data, ensuring users don't need to exert effort searching for or sharing information.

Remote Expert Support

Trained experts use the user's preferred language to address health concerns — solving language barriers and providing reliable, in-person information.

Label Translation

Instantly translates product labels and descriptions into the user's native language, enabling confident purchase decisions.

Service Components

A complete end-to-end experience — from home country to pharmacy kiosk.

Pre-departure test kit — stores health data before travel.

Portable smart card case — stores medical records and detects environmental changes.

In-pharmacy kiosks — connect with the device to deliver support and information.

Reflections

What I learned along the way.

Empathy-Driven Design

This project reinforced how important it is to go beyond assumptions and truly listen to users. Pharmacy shopping expectations vary across nationalities. Hearing users' stories helped me understand their perspectives, step into their shoes, and uncover deeper pain points.

Thinking Beyond the Screen

Initially, I focused on in-store challenges, but deeper research revealed that the problem began long before students even reached the pharmacy. Great UX isn't just about solving isolated issues — it's about designing seamless, end-to-end experiences.

Finding Possibilities Within Constraints

Due to project constraints, I had to explore solutions beyond a mobile app. While challenging, this pushed me to think creatively. I believe a mobile application would be the more effective and feasible long-term solution.

The Power of Authentic User Insights

Recruiting participants was tougher than expected, but every conversation brought the project to life in ways no secondary research ever could. Hearing personal stories validated assumptions, shattered others, and surfaced insights that completely reshaped my approach.
